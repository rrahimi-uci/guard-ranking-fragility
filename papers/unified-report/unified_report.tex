\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{The goal.} When a practitioner fine-tunes a small language model into a binary safety
\emph{guard} and reports its benchmark score, that number reads like a property of the guard. It is not.
This report shows --- precisely, and at four scales --- that a guard's score is \emph{co-produced}: by the
benchmark it is read on, the training objective, the decision threshold, and the domain. Establishing this
requires a fair yardstick, so we measure on one fixed panel of four compact instruction checkpoints
(Qwen2.5-1.5B, SmolLM2-1.7B, SmolLM3-3B, Qwen3-4B) and compare each guard only against its \emph{own}
pre-fine-tuning base, on identical rows, prompts, and scoring --- attributing every change to the
fine-tune, not to a difference between models.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{(I) Fine-tuning specializes; it does not transfer.} A standard LoRA supervised fine-tune
(SFT) lifts ranking on training-\emph{represented} sources to a near-common ceiling ($\approx\!0.98$
macro-AP; $\RepDelta$ on average) but on \emph{held-out} datasets changes transfer by only $\TransferDelta$
on average --- an average that hides opposite per-checkpoint signs, from $+0.04$ to $-0.15$. We show this is
an \emph{attractor}: SFT pulls every checkpoint to a benchmark-fixed endpoint (transfer $0.807\!\pm\!0.024$
across the panel), so the tempting reading ``stronger bases specialize more'' is \emph{forced arithmetic}
(the change slopes $-1$ in the base score, $R^2\!=\!0.91$), not a behavioral law. At a deployable threshold
the specialized guard raises transfer false alarms ($\TransferBaseFPRPct\%\!\to\!\TransferSFTFPRPct\%$) and
\emph{loses} hard-attack recall ($\HarmBenchBaseRecallPct\%\!\to\!\HarmBenchSFTRecallPct\%$).
\textbf{(II) The objective is an axis, not a detail.} A locked pre-registration asks whether SFT vs.\ DPO
vs.\ GRPO changes the trade-off; a single-token argument predicts a bounded GRPO null, and the result
awaits the locked run.
\textbf{(III) Composition recovers transfer without retraining.} Averaging the base's and the fine-tune's
\emph{calibrated} scores lifts transfer over SFT for all four checkpoints ($\PilotTransferDeltaSFT$) as an
ensemble diversity gain --- but it is recovery, not dominance (it can sit below the untuned base), and it
restores no transferable threshold.
\textbf{(IV) A change of domain hides the violation.} A general-safety score does not cover
regulated-domain compliance. An HMDA-grounded, \emph{dual-labeled} mortgage benchmark isolates requests
that read as safe yet violate policy (the load-bearing \textbf{G0/D1} stratum) and adds a protected-class
fairness-invariance gate; an external, \emph{expert-annotated} set (ExpGuard, 2{,}275 rows) extends the
test to \textbf{finance, health, and law}, where all four base guards rank violations well zero-shot
(AP $0.88$--$0.96$) but the best is SmolLM3-3B --- not the largest model, and a different winner than the
mortgage benchmark picks.

\smallskip
\noindent The recurring character is \textbf{Qwen3-4B}: the strongest base, the one that specializes
\emph{most}, the one composition helps \emph{least}, yet the best and fairest zero-shot \emph{mortgage}
guard --- the ranking flips with the benchmark. All evidence is \emph{retrospective and estimation-only}
on a fixed panel (mortgage labels are LLM-judge, not expert-adjudicated), so no causal, universal,
deployment, or fair-lending claim is licensed. Every number, table, and figure regenerates from committed
per-row scores through one \code{reproduce} entry point --- nothing is hand-typed --- and all code, data
manifests, and the frozen benchmark are public
(\Cref{sec:repro}; \url{https://github.com/rrahimi-uci/guard-ranking-fragility}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: why ``is this guard good?'' is the wrong question}
\label{sec:intro}

\begin{background}{In one paragraph}
A ``guard'' is a small model that reads an incoming request and labels it \code{safe} or \code{unsafe}
before an assistant acts on it. The common recipe is: take a general chat model, fine-tune it into a
guard, and report its benchmark score --- usually compared against \emph{other} models. That hides the
quantity a practitioner actually needs: what did the fine-tune change relative to the \emph{same model
before tuning}, and does that change survive on data the guard never trained on? This report answers
that at three scales (does tuning transfer? does the \emph{objective} matter? can we compose to
recover?) and then asks whether any of it holds where it matters most --- \emph{regulated domains}
where a violation can read as perfectly polite text.
\end{background}

Converting a general instruction model into a safety guard with a short parameter-efficient fine-tune
\citep{elesedy2024loraguard} and reporting its score on a public suite \citep{bassani2024guardbench}
is now standard. A post-tuning leaderboard number, however, conflates two different things: raising the
score on benchmark \emph{sources represented in training} versus improving \emph{transfer} to datasets
the guard never saw. This report is organized around one thesis --- \emph{the benchmark co-produces the
verdict} --- and four questions on a single shared panel of checkpoints:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Does fine-tuning transfer?} (\Cref{sec:actI}) --- or does the guard specialize to its training sources?
  \item \textbf{Does the objective matter?} (\Cref{sec:actII}) --- SFT vs DPO vs GRPO. \textsc{pending} the locked run.
  \item \textbf{Can we recover transfer without retraining?} (\Cref{sec:actIII}) --- by composing base $+$ adapter.
  \item \textbf{Does any of it survive a change of domain?} (\Cref{sec:actIV}) --- across law, finance, health, and mortgage.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{What is new here.} Prior work shows fine-tuning can weaken safety behavior
\citep{hsiung2025guardrails}, that guards overfit their training policy
\citep{liu2026domaingeneralizable}, and that a plain instruction model can rival a purpose-built guard
\citep{bassani2024guardbench} --- but those compare \emph{different} models, which confounds ``the
fine-tune changed the guard'' with ``the two models differ.'' Our through-line removes that confound with
a \emph{paired, same-checkpoint} measurement and then pushes it across objectives, a composition remedy,
and four domains. Concretely, the novel contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.7em,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{A paired estimand} that cleanly separates represented-source gain from held-out transfer,
  with a fixed-panel hierarchical bootstrap (\Cref{sec:actI}).
  \item \textbf{The ``attractor'' finding:} SFT drives every checkpoint to a benchmark-fixed endpoint, so
  the popular reading ``stronger bases specialize more'' is arithmetic; the sharper, falsifiable claim is
  \emph{endpoint invariance} (\Cref{sec:actI-coupling}).
  \item \textbf{A pre-registered objective axis} (SFT/DPO/GRPO) with a single-token argument for why GRPO
  should end KL-close (\Cref{sec:actII}), committed before the run as an anti-HARKing timestamp.
  \item \textbf{A retraining-free composition operator} that recovers transfer as an ensemble diversity
  gain, with the mechanism (base-correlated adapter errors) made explicit (\Cref{sec:actIII}).
  \item \textbf{A dual-labeled, HMDA-grounded mortgage benchmark} ($G\times D$) with a protected-class
  fairness-invariance gate, plus an external, expert-annotated finance/health/law replication
  (\Cref{sec:actIV}).
  \item \textbf{A reproduce-from-committed-scores pipeline:} one entry point regenerates every table and
  figure and asserts byte-identity against the paper (\Cref{sec:repro}).
\end{enumerate}
\emph{What is \textbf{not} new:} we introduce no new model, metric, or training algorithm, and make no
causal, universal, or deployment claim --- the output is a reproducible, estimation-only characterization
of this fixed panel plus one external domain replication.

\paragraph{Scope.} English input-prompt classification; binary \code{safe}/\code{unsafe}; four compact
checkpoints (1.5--4B); one LoRA recipe; objectives SFT/DPO/GRPO; four regulated domains. Not a
leaderboard, not a fair-lending audit, not a deployment recommendation.

\paragraph{Result --- external breadth (finance/health/law).} On ExpGuard's expert-annotated prompts,
all four base checkpoints already rank domain violations well \emph{zero-shot} (aggregate AP
$0.88$--$0.96$; \Cref{tab:expguard}, \Cref{fig:expguard-domains}). The ordering echoes Act~I's lesson
rather than raw capacity: \textbf{SmolLM3-3B is the strongest domain guard} (AP $0.956$, near-uniform
across the three verticals), with Qwen3-4B a close second ($0.951$), while the smaller checkpoints trail
(Qwen2.5-1.5B $0.921$, SmolLM2-1.7B $0.883$) --- the ranking is \emph{not} monotone in parameter count.
As external, expert-labeled evidence these numbers sit at a strictly stronger label tier than the mortgage
LLM-judge, so they are reported \emph{beside}, and never pooled with, the mortgage or retrospective-panel
numbers. The ranking score is the raw decision margin $z_{\text{unsafe}}-z_{\text{safe}}$ (not a saturating
probability), so \code{eval\_expguard\_external.py --from-scores} reproduces every entry exactly from the
committed text-free per-row scores; the run was independently cross-checked on an L4 GPU and Apple MPS
(agreement to $3$--$4$ decimals).

\section{Synthesis --- one lesson at four scales}
\label{sec:synthesis}
The four acts are one lesson. A guard's benchmark score is co-produced by the benchmark: SFT buys
represented-source ranking, not transfer (Act I); the specialization is a property to measure across
\emph{objectives}, not assume (Act II); keeping the base in an output-space average \emph{recovers} some
transfer for free of retraining, though not a threshold (Act III); and a change of domain can hide the
violation entirely, so regulated domains need domain-grounded evaluation and a fairness gate (Act IV).
The recurring cast makes it concrete: Qwen3-4B, the strongest base, specializes the most on transfer,
is the one composition hurts, yet is the best and fairest zero-shot mortgage guard --- the ranking
flips with the benchmark.

\section{The practitioner's decision guide}
\label{sec:practitioner}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Always compare to your own base}, on represented \emph{and} held-out sets, plus a
  benign over-refusal and a hard-attack stress set --- a leaderboard delta vs.\ other models hides
  specialization.
  \item \textbf{Expect an objective to specialize} unless measured otherwise (Act II); don't assume RL
  ``generalizes'' on a single-token guard.
  \item \textbf{If transfer matters, try composition before tuning harder} --- but re-calibrate the
  threshold on the target regime (ranking recovery $\neq$ calibration transfer).
  \item \textbf{In a regulated domain, build domain-grounded, dual-labeled evaluation} and a
  protected-pair invariance check; a general-safety score does not cover compliance.
\end{enumerate}
Each step carries its caveat: these are estimates on a fixed panel, and the domain labels are a
measuring stick, not a verdict.

\section{Reproducibility}
\label{sec:repro}
Every number above is \code{\textbackslash input} from a committed generated table bound to a lock ---
none is hand-typed. A single entry point, \code{make reproduce} (\code{papers/unified-report/reproduce.py}),
regenerates all tables/figures from committed per-row scores by calling each study's analysis (Paper A in
its lock-pinned env; Paper B, mortgage, ExpGuard, and Paper C from committed scores), and asserts
byte-identity with the committed report tables. It needs no GPU and no network; only re-training the
guards or re-scoring the gated ExpGuard set requires a GPU / dataset access. Raw third-party rows are
referenced by pinned identifier + revision + content hash, not redistributed.

\paragraph{Code and data availability.} All code, data manifests, generated tables, and the frozen
benchmark are public at \url{https://github.com/rrahimi-uci/guard-ranking-fragility}. The one-command
pipeline is \code{papers/unified-report/reproduce.py} (\code{make reproduce}); the frozen, dual-labeled
mortgage benchmark ships at \code{mortgage-benchmark/benchmark/v1\_hmda2022/} (994 rows,
SHA-256-checksummed, with a text-free index); the committed per-row guard scores that regenerate every
number live under \code{artifacts/} (Paper~A in \code{artifacts/paper\_a\_sft\_v2/}, the mortgage baseline
in \code{mortgage-benchmark/out\_eval/}, and the finance/health/law scores in
\code{artifacts/expguard\_external/}); the figures are built by \code{papers/unified-report/figures/make\_figures.py};
and the Act~II objective-axis pre-registration and trainer are \code{docs/paper-c-prereg.md} and
\code{experiments/run\_paper\_c\_objective.py}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
A small guard's benchmark score is not a fixed property of the guard --- the benchmark co-produces it.
Measured honestly, fine-tuning specializes rather than uniformly improves; the effect is worth measuring
across objectives; a retraining-free composition recovers some of the lost transfer; and in regulated
domains a general-safety score does not cover compliance. The disciplined next step is not a more
confident headline but a prospectively locked evaluation on genuinely uninspected data.
